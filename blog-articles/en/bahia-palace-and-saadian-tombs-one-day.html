Yes — and it's one of the best half-days you can spend in Marrakech. Bahia Palace and the Saadian Tombs are 7 minutes apart on foot. They offer completely different architectural experiences, and together they cover 400 years of Moroccan history across a single compact route in the southern medina. Morocco welcomed 17.4 million visitors in 2024 (ONMT, Morocco Tourism Annual Report 2024), and this corner of the medina absorbs a significant share of them — which makes timing your visit correctly important. Here's exactly how to do it.

Key Takeaways

  • Bahia Palace and the Saadian Tombs are 650 metres apart: a 7 to 10 minute walk through the Mellah district. The combined visit, done properly, takes 2.5 to 3 hours and is comfortably finished by noon.
  • Start with Bahia Palace. It opens at 9:00 AM, has a longer queue at the door, takes more time to explore, and benefits most from the quiet of the first 90 minutes. The tombs are smaller, absorb crowds better, and work well as the second site at 10:30 AM.
  • The total ticket cost for both is 170 MAD per foreign adult — roughly €15 for access to two of the most significant monuments in Morocco. Both are cash-only at the door.

How Far Apart Are Bahia Palace and the Saadian Tombs?

  • Walking time: 7 to 10 minutes between the two entrances
  • Distance: approximately 650 metres
  • Route: from Bahia Palace, head south through the Mellah district, following signs for the Saadian Tombs — they're well posted in French and Arabic. The walk itself passes through the Mellah, Marrakech's old Jewish quarter, and is worth the slow pace.

Getting to Bahia Palace in the first place: see the walking directions from Jemaa el-Fna. The standard route (Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim → Rue Riad Zitoun el Jedid) is about 15 minutes from the main square. Download an offline Google Maps before you leave your accommodation.

Quick Overview of Each Site

Bahia Palace

A 19th-century royal palace with a total complex of roughly 8 hectares (approximately 8,000 m² open to visitors) — grand courtyards, harem quarters, painted cedar ceilings, zellige tilework, and gardens. Built by Grand Vizier Ba Ahmed between 1894 and 1900, managed today by Morocco's Ministry of Culture (minculture.gov.ma). Entry: 100 MAD for foreign adults. Time needed: 60 to 90 minutes. Children under 7 free; foreign children 7–13 pay 50 MAD. Full breakdown on the entrance fee page.

Saadian Tombs

A 16th-century royal mausoleum built for the Saadian dynasty under Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur. Three chambers of extraordinary carved plasterwork and zellige surround the burial plots of 66 members of the royal family. Sealed by Sultan Moulay Ismail in the 17th century and only rediscovered in 1917 — the site had been hidden for over 200 years. Entry: 70 MAD. Time needed: 30 to 45 minutes.

Which to Visit First?

Start with Bahia Palace.

The logic is straightforward. Bahia Palace opens at 9:00 AM and the first 90 minutes are consistently the quietest of the day — see the timing guide for why the 9:00 AM window matters so much. The palace also has a single ticket window where the queue can reach 30 to 45 minutes in peak season: arriving at opening (or with a pre-booked ticket) removes this problem entirely.

The Saadian Tombs also open at 9:00 AM, but the site is much smaller. It absorbs crowds better than the palace's long corridors and single entrance. Starting at the palace means you get the larger, more complex site when it's emptiest and move to the tombs once you've had the full benefit of the quiet morning.

The other reason: Bahia Palace takes longer. Visitors who do the tombs first often rush through the palace to avoid arriving when it's crowded — and end up getting the worst version of both. Do the palace first, spend 90 minutes, and walk to the tombs when the crowds there are still manageable.

Both sites are cash only at the door. Bring dirhams. If you've booked a ticket for Bahia Palace online, you can pay by card for that — but have cash ready for the Saadian Tombs ticket window. The nearest reliable ATMs are on Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim, on your walk from Jemaa el-Fna.

Where Should You Eat Between Bahia Palace and the Saadian Tombs?

The honest answer: don't eat between them. At 90 minutes per site with a 10-minute walk between, you'll be done by noon. A proper lunch after both sites makes more sense than a rushed snack midway through the morning.

If you need something between the two:

  • Café near Place des Ferblantiers (3 minutes west of the route): several small cafés on and around the square serve mint tea and simple snacks. 15 MAD for tea, 30 to 50 MAD for a sandwich.
  • Mellah market stalls: fresh orange juice squeezed to order (10–15 MAD) is available on the walk through the Mellah. Not a meal, but excellent hydration on a warm morning.

For lunch after both sites: head back north toward Jemaa el-Fna via Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim. Several good tagine restaurants along this route serve honest meals at 60 to 120 MAD for a main. Ask for a recommendation at your riad rather than picking the first tourist-facing restaurant you see.

Should You Add El Badi Palace for a Full Morning?

Yes, if you have until 13:00. El Badi Palace is 10 minutes west of the Saadian Tombs, near Place des Ferblantiers. Entry is 70 MAD. The ruins are atmospheric: vast roofless halls, nesting storks on the towers, and an open-air scale that is very different from the intimate Saadian Tombs. It adds about 45 minutes and rounds out the southern medina's three major monuments in a single morning.

Total ticket cost for all three: 240 MAD per foreign adult (roughly €22). Three of Morocco's most significant monuments for the price of one entry at Majorelle Garden. The Bahia Palace vs El Badi Palace comparison covers what each site offers in more detail.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are the Saadian Tombs and Bahia Palace near each other?

Yes — they are approximately 650 metres apart, a 7 to 10 minute walk through the southern medina. The route is straightforward: exit Bahia Palace, head south through the Mellah district, and follow the signs for the Saadian Tombs. They're well posted in French and Arabic.

Which is better — the Saadian Tombs or Bahia Palace?

They're not comparable — they offer different experiences. The Saadian Tombs are smaller, more concentrated, and intensely detailed in a compact space: three chambers of carving and tilework around royal burial plots. Bahia Palace is vast, varied, and architectural in scope: 8,000 m² of courtyards, reception halls, gardens, and harem quarters. If you only have time for one, Bahia Palace offers more variety and a longer, richer visit. If you have 2.5 to 3 hours, do both.

Do I need to book tickets for the Saadian Tombs in advance?

There is no official advance booking system for the Saadian Tombs: tickets are purchased at the entrance (70 MAD cash). The queue is usually shorter than Bahia Palace's and moves faster, as the site is smaller. Arriving before 10:00 AM keeps the wait minimal. For Bahia Palace, an advance ticket is strongly recommended in peak season (March through November).
