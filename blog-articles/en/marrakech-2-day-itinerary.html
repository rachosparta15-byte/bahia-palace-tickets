Two days is enough to see the best of Marrakech without rushing: the medina monuments, the garden escapes, the souks, and Jemaa el-Fna at night. Morocco's National Tourism Office recorded 17.4 million international arrivals in 2024 (ONMT, Morocco Tourism Annual Report 2024), making Marrakech one of the most concentrated visitor cities in North Africa. Getting the sequence right — which site first, when to arrive, what to skip — is the difference between a great 48 hours and a hot, crowded one. This itinerary gives you real timings, honest prices, and a sequence that makes logistical sense.

Key Takeaways

  • Start Day 1 at Bahia Palace at 9:00 AM. Every major site in Marrakech peaks with crowds between 10:30 and 14:00. Arriving at opening at the city's most complex monument means you experience it at its best before the tour buses arrive.
  • Book in advance where possible. Bahia Palace and Majorelle Garden both have notoriously slow ticket queues in peak season. Pre-booking saves 30 to 45 minutes of standing in direct sun at each site.
  • Budget per person for activities (excluding accommodation): Bahia Palace (100 MAD) + Saadian Tombs (70 MAD) + Majorelle Garden (150 MAD) + Ben Youssef Madrasa (70 MAD) = 390 MAD in monument entry fees across 2 days.

Day 1 Morning: Where Should You Start in Marrakech?

08:50 — Arrive at Bahia Palace

Get there 5 minutes before the 9:00 AM opening. If you've booked a ticket online, you walk straight to the entrance when the gates open. If not, join the ticket queue — it's shortest right at opening, but in peak season even the early queue can take 15 to 20 minutes.

Inside the palace: start with the Grand Riad courtyard, work through the reception rooms, spend time in the harem quarters, and finish in the gardens. See the room-by-room guide for exactly what to look for in each section. Budget 75 to 90 minutes. The timing guide explains why this exact window matters.

What to notice: the hand-painted cedar ceilings (no two panels are identical), the zellige floor patterns in the Grand Riad, and the scale of the main courtyard relative to what you've been walking through in the medina. Read the hidden details guide the night before for the specific things most visitors walk past.

10:30 — Walk to Saadian Tombs (7 minutes)

Head south from Bahia Palace through the Mellah district. Follow the signs (well-posted in French and Arabic). The Saadian Tombs entrance is on a small lane just past the Moulay El Yazid Mosque.

Inside the tombs: three chambers surround the burial plots of 66 members of the Saadian royal family. The Hall of Twelve Columns is the highlight — Italian marble columns, carved plaster at its most intricate, and an atmosphere that is genuinely different from Bahia Palace's grandeur. The site was sealed in the 17th century and not rediscovered until 1917. Budget 35 to 45 minutes. Entry: 70 MAD cash. The combined Saadian Tombs guide covers everything about this half-day route.

11:30 — Optional: El Badi Palace (10 min walk)

If you have the energy and time, El Badi Palace is worth 45 minutes. Head west from the Saadian Tombs toward Place des Ferblantiers. The ruins of this once-magnificent 16th-century palace are now open roofless grounds with nesting storks and an atmosphere unlike anything else in the medina. Entry: 70 MAD. See the Bahia vs El Badi comparison for what each site offers.

Day 1 Afternoon: Which Souks Are Worth Your Time?

12:30 — Lunch

Head back through the medina toward Jemaa el-Fna. Along Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim, several small restaurants serve tagines and couscous at honest prices (60 to 100 MAD for a main). The rooftop restaurants on the north side of Jemaa el-Fna give you views over the square while you eat, at slightly higher prices.

14:00 — The Souks

Enter the main souk area north of Jemaa el-Fna. The relevant sections:

  • Souk Smarine — the main covered souk; leather goods, textiles, and lanterns
  • Souk Cherratine — the leather workers' quarter; bags, belts, shoes
  • Souk Sabbaghin — the dyers' souk; dyed wool hanging to dry in vivid colours
  • Rahba Kedima (Spice Square) — herbs, spices, and a hard sell from the vendors

Allow 90 minutes to walk through without specific shopping intention. If you're buying, add time. Negotiate everything — the opening price in tourist-facing souks is typically 2 to 3 times the fair price. Start at 30% of what's quoted and meet somewhere in the middle. See the safety guide for scam avoidance in the souk area.

Day 1 Evening: What Makes Jemaa el-Fna Worth Visiting at Night?

18:00 — Return to Jemaa el-Fna

The square transforms completely at dusk. Storytellers, musicians, acrobats, and food stall operators all appear simultaneously. The energy is loud, chaotic, and completely unlike anything you'll find elsewhere. Even seasoned Morocco travellers find the transformation between the daytime and the evening versions of Jemaa el-Fna genuinely striking.

Practical: keep your bags in front of you. The food stalls (numbered 1–100) are safe to eat from: grilled meats, harira soup, snails, and fresh-squeezed orange juice. A full meal costs 80 to 150 MAD. The rooftop restaurants surrounding the square offer the same view with a calmer setting and more structured menu.

Day 2 Morning: Majorelle Garden + YSL Museum

09:00 — Majorelle Garden

Book your Majorelle Garden ticket online before you go: the walk-in queue is significant in high season. Entry is 150 MAD for the garden, or 200 MAD with the Berber Museum. The famous cobalt blue villa against lush greenery, the cactus collection, and the lotus ponds make it one of the most photographed places in Morocco. Budget 60 to 75 minutes for the garden. The YSL Museum next door (separate ticket, 100 MAD) is worth an additional 45 minutes if you have an interest in fashion or design. See the Bahia Palace vs Majorelle comparison for how the two sites differ.

10:30 — Gueliz Neighbourhood

Majorelle sits in Gueliz, Marrakech's French-built new town. It's a different city from the medina: wide boulevards, European café architecture, and a noticeably lower-pressure atmosphere. Walk Avenue Mohammed V for 20 minutes and have coffee at one of the European-style cafés. The contrast with the medina is striking and worth pausing to appreciate.

Is Ben Youssef Madrasa Worth Adding to Your Itinerary?

12:00 — Lunch in Gueliz

The restaurant options in Gueliz are better value and less tourist-oriented than Jemaa el-Fna. A good local tagine restaurant in this area costs 80 to 130 MAD for a main. Ask your accommodation for a specific recommendation.

14:00 — Ben Youssef Madrasa

Yes, absolutely worth adding. A 14th-century Quranic school in the northern medina and one of the finest examples of Islamic architecture in Morocco. Three floors of carved stucco and zellige surround a central courtyard in a more compact and contemplative version of what Bahia Palace offers. Entry: 70 MAD. Budget 45 minutes. The Ben Youssef Madrasa is less visited than Bahia Palace or Majorelle and often genuinely quiet by early afternoon.

15:00 — Final Souk Pass or Rest

The area around Ben Youssef Madrasa has some of the better craft shops in Marrakech: less tourist-oriented than the main souk strip. Pottery, leather, and textile workshops at more realistic prices with less pressure. A good area to make thoughtful purchases that you wouldn't make under time pressure at the main souks.

Day 2 Evening: Rooftop Dinner

19:00 — Rooftop Dinner

Marrakech has excellent rooftop restaurants within the medina. A typical riad dinner with a set menu costs 200 to 400 MAD per person including starter, main, dessert, and mint tea. Book in advance: the best ones fill up in spring and autumn. Alternatively, return to Jemaa el-Fna for a second evening at the stalls.

What Should You Know Before Planning This Itinerary?

  • Transport: Most of Day 1 is walkable within the medina. Use petit taxis for the 3 km trip between the medina and Gueliz/Majorelle. Agree on the fare before getting in (20 to 35 MAD is fair). Careem and inDrive apps give upfront pricing and are more reliable than street-hailed taxis.
  • Dress code: Bahia Palace, the Saadian Tombs, and Ben Youssef Madrasa all require covered shoulders and knees. Dress appropriately for the full day and you'll have no issues at any site. See the dress code guide for what works in the heat.
  • Cash: All monument ticket windows are cash only. Have dirhams available at all times. ATMs inside the medina are limited: withdraw from a bank ATM before entering the old city.
  • Safety: Marrakech is safe for tourists. The main irritant is unsolicited "guides" near the monuments. A firm "no thank you" once is enough. Don't follow anyone who approaches you unsolicited. The safety guide covers the specific situations to watch for.
  • Heat: In summer (June through September), schedule outdoor activities before 11:00 AM and after 17:00. The medina's covered lanes provide shade but the heat is real. Bring water for each person.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 2 days enough for Marrakech?

Two days is enough to see the main highlights: Bahia Palace, Saadian Tombs, Majorelle Garden, Ben Youssef Madrasa, the souks, and Jemaa el-Fna. It doesn't leave time for day trips (Atlas Mountains, Essaouira) or deeper exploration of the medina. A third day adds significantly more breathing room.

What's the best season for a 2-day Marrakech trip?

March through May and September through November are the best months: temperatures are comfortable (20–26°C), all sites are open, and the city is active without summer's extreme heat. December through February is quiet and cool but pleasant. July through August is hot (38–42°C) but manageable if you start each day early.

How much does 2 days in Marrakech cost?

Accommodation varies enormously (500–2,000 MAD per night for a riad). Activities for 2 days total roughly 500–600 MAD per person for the main monuments. Food costs 300–500 MAD per person per day depending on where you eat. Total budget excluding flights and accommodation: approximately 1,100–2,000 MAD (~€100–180) per person for 2 days of activities and meals.
