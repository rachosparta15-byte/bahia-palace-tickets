# How to Get to Bahia Palace from Jemaa el-Fna (Walking, Taxi & More)

Bahia Palace is in the southern medina of Marrakech — which sounds simple until you're actually inside the medina, surrounded by a labyrinth of unlabeled alleyways, wrong turns, and very confident locals pointing you in different directions. First-timers often find it harder to reach than expected. The directions aren't complicated, but the medina doesn't behave like a normal city grid, and that catches people off guard. This guide gives you a clear, step-by-step route from the main square at Jemaa el-Fna, plus alternatives for those who don't want to walk.

---

## Bahia Palace Address & Location

**Bahia Palace** sits in the Mellah district of Marrakech's old medina, southeast of the central square.

**Address:** Rue Riad Zitoun el Jedid, Marrakech 40000, Morocco

**GPS Coordinates:** 31.6226° N, 7.9827° W

The palace is not visible from any main road. Its entrance is on a street that runs between two larger medina arteries. First-time visitors often walk past it — the entrance is elegant but understated, marked by heavy wooden doors rather than a large signposted gateway.

## Walking from Jemaa el-Fna (~15 Minutes)

Walking is the best way to reach Bahia Palace from Jemaa el-Fna. The route takes you through the living, working medina — past spice sellers, leather goods, and tea shops — and is part of the experience of being in Marrakech. The distance is roughly 1.2 kilometers.

**Step-by-step walking directions:**

1. **Start at Jemaa el-Fna** with the Koutoubia Mosque behind you (to the west). Face east, toward the medina.

2. **Enter the medina** via the main souks entrance or the street called **Rue Mouassine** on the north side, OR take the more direct southern route down **Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim** (look for signs; this street heads directly south from the eastern edge of the square).

3. **Walk south along Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim.** This is a wide-ish covered street by medina standards. Keep going south — about 10 minutes of walking. You'll pass souvenir shops, small cafes, and craftsmen's workshops.

4. **Look for the fork.** Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim meets Rue Riad Zitoun el Jedid further south. Stay on this main corridor heading south.

5. **Watch for the palace sign.** Bahia Palace will appear on your **left side**, marked by a sign in Arabic and French and the large wooden entrance doors. If you reach the Mellah (Jewish quarter market) you've gone slightly too far — double back north about 100 meters.

**Total walking time:** 12–18 minutes depending on pace and crowds.

**Tip:** Download the Google Maps offline map for Marrakech before you leave your accommodation. The medina is mapped reasonably well and having GPS while offline is far more reliable than asking for directions in a busy street.

## Taking a Taxi or Caleche

### Petit Taxi (Small Red Taxi)

Marrakech's petit taxis are small red cars (Fiats and Dacias typically) that run on meters — or should. In practice, many drivers negotiate a fixed fare with tourists rather than using the meter.

**Reasonable fare from Jemaa el-Fna to Bahia Palace:** 15–25 MAD by meter. Fixed fare negotiations will typically start at 50–80 MAD from a tourist starting point — negotiate down, and agree on the price before you get in.

**Practical notes:**
- Petit taxis cannot enter the deepest lanes of the medina. They'll drop you at the nearest accessible point, usually on Rue Riad Zitoun el Jedid, a 2–3 minute walk from the entrance.
- Tell the driver "Palais Bahia" — it's well known.
- If a driver claims not to know where it is, find another driver.

### Caleche (Horse-Drawn Carriage)

Caleches are a distinctly Marrakech option — large horse-drawn carriages that carry tourists around the city walls and to major attractions. They operate from ranks near Jemaa el-Fna.

**Fare to Bahia Palace:** Negotiate before boarding. Expect 60–100 MAD for the trip one way. The ride itself is pleasant and slow — a caleche through the medina edge is an experience, not just transport.

**Limitation:** Caleches cannot navigate the narrow medina lanes. They'll drop you at the closest accessible street, adding a short walk.

### Ride Apps

Both **Careem** and **inDrive** operate in Marrakech and can be more reliable for price transparency than street-hailing taxis. If you have a local SIM or working mobile data, booking through an app shows you the fare upfront. This is especially useful for the return journey when you're tired and less inclined to negotiate.

## Using Google Maps in the Medina

Google Maps works in the Marrakech medina — better than many people expect, actually. The key is using it correctly:

**Before you leave your accommodation:**
- Download the offline map of Marrakech (in the Google Maps app: search Marrakech → Download → select the area)
- Save Bahia Palace as a saved place so you can navigate to it without a search

**While navigating:**
- Set your route to walking — driving directions in the medina are often useless since most lanes are pedestrian-only
- Zoom in when you're within 200 meters of the destination — some lanes aren't perfectly labeled and a small zoom discrepancy can send you to the wrong alley
- If the app says you've arrived but you can't see the entrance, look for the large wooden doors on your left; you may be standing nearly at them

**Alternative: use what3words.** The app assigns every 3x3 meter square in the world a unique three-word address. The entrance to Bahia Palace has its own what3words address — a useful backup if GPS gets confused in dense alleyways.

## Parking Nearby (If Driving)

Driving into the medina is not practical — the lanes are too narrow and mostly pedestrian-only. If you're arriving by car, park outside the medina walls and walk or take a short taxi to the palace.

**Closest parking options:**
- **Parking El Badi:** A large surface car park near El Badi Palace on Place des Ferblantiers, roughly 600 meters west of Bahia Palace. Free or very low cost.
- **Parking Mellah:** A smaller car park just south of the Mellah market area, very close to the palace entrance.

From either car park, walking to the palace entrance takes 5–10 minutes. The streets in this part of the southern medina are slightly less congested than the main tourist souk area, making the walk manageable.

Car parks in Marrakech's medina area are typically informal — a guardian will collect a small fee (5–10 MAD) when you leave. This is normal and expected; it's not a scam.

## What's Near Bahia Palace?

Once you've visited the palace, these sites are all within walking distance:

### Saadian Tombs (~10 minutes south)
The royal mausoleum of the Saadian dynasty, sealed for centuries and rediscovered in 1917. Small, remarkably detailed, and well worth 45 minutes. Follow the signs south from Bahia Palace through the Mellah.

### El Badi Palace (~15 minutes west)
The dramatic ruins of a 16th-century palace complex — vast roofless halls and stork-topped towers. A very different atmosphere from Bahia. Head west from Bahia Palace toward Place des Ferblantiers.

### Mellah (Jewish Quarter)
Immediately adjacent to Bahia Palace, the Mellah is Marrakech's old Jewish quarter. The covered market streets here are less touristy than the main souks and worth a wander. The architecture is distinct — taller buildings, wrought-iron balconies.

### Rue Riad Zitoun el Jedid (The Street Itself)
The street leading to the palace is lined with craft workshops, small restaurants, and local shops. Less aggressive than the tourist-facing souks north of Jemaa el-Fna, this area rewards slow walking.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How far is Bahia Palace from Jemaa el-Fna?**
Bahia Palace is approximately 1.2 kilometers from Jemaa el-Fna. On foot through the medina, the walk takes 12–18 minutes depending on your pace and how busy the streets are. By petit taxi, it's a 5–10 minute ride costing 15–25 MAD by meter.

**Q: Can I walk to Bahia Palace from the medina?**
Yes, and walking is the recommended way to get there. The route along Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim and Rue Riad Zitoun el Jedid is straightforward and takes you through genuine medina neighborhoods rather than the more tourist-saturated souk area. Download Google Maps offline before your trip for navigation confidence.

**Q: Is there a bus to Bahia Palace?**
Marrakech's city buses (ALSA) don't run routes into the heart of the medina. The nearest stops are on the medina's perimeter roads. From Jemaa el-Fna, walking or taking a petit taxi is faster and more practical than using a bus for this specific journey. Ride-hailing apps (Careem, inDrive) are a good alternative to street taxis if you prefer upfront pricing.
