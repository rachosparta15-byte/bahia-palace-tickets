# 10 Things to Know Before Visiting Bahia Palace Marrakech

Bahia Palace is one of the most visited monuments in Morocco — and one of the most misunderstood. Most tourists know it as "the palace in Marrakech," but very few arrive knowing what they're actually looking at, what to prioritize, or how to avoid the most common trip-ruining mistakes. Whether this is your first time in Morocco or you've been before, these ten things will make your visit genuinely better.

---

## 1. Book Your Tickets in Advance

This is the single most impactful thing you can do before you visit. Bahia Palace has one ticket window. During peak season — spring (March–May) and autumn (September–November) — queues at that window can take 30 to 45 minutes. In summer, with heat adding to the discomfort, it's worse.

A [skip-the-line ticket](/en/tickets/skip-the-line) lets you bypass the queue entirely. You arrive, show your confirmation, and walk in. The price difference is minimal. The difference in experience is significant.

Book at least 24–48 hours in advance outside peak season. During high season or around public holidays, book 3–5 days ahead. Don't assume you can sort it on the day — time slots fill up.

---

## 2. Go in the Early Morning

Bahia Palace opens at 9:00 AM. If you can be there at opening, do it. The first 90 minutes are the quietest of the day.

Here's what changes when you go early:
- No tour groups yet (they typically arrive from 10:30 onwards)
- Better light in the open courtyards for photography — softer, directional, not harsh overhead midday glare
- Room to stand still and look at the carved plasterwork without someone walking into frame
- Cooler temperatures — Marrakech mornings are noticeably more comfortable than midday, especially June through September

Going early isn't just advice for photography enthusiasts. Even if you never take a photo, being able to walk through a historic palace at your own pace — without being nudged along by a crowd — is a fundamentally different experience.

---

## 3. Wear Comfortable, Sensible Shoes

Bahia Palace covers approximately 8,000 square meters across multiple courtyards, gardens, reception halls, and corridors. You will walk on:
- Polished zellige tile floors (beautiful; also slippery)
- Uneven stone passages between sections
- Slightly raised thresholds between rooms

Heels are a bad idea. Flip-flops with no grip are a liability. Wear flat, closed-toe shoes or sandals with a proper sole. Sneakers are perfect. If you're a photographer who plans to spend time crouching for low-angle shots, something with ankle support is worth thinking about.

You do not need to remove your shoes — Bahia Palace is not a mosque. But comfort over the course of 1.5–2 hours of walking matters.

---

## 4. Bring Water (and Cash)

**Water:** Marrakech is hot. Even in spring and autumn, temperatures climb to 25–30°C by midday. Inside the palace, shaded courtyards help, but there's no café or water station once you're in. Bring a bottle. Staying hydrated throughout your time in the medina is basic survival, not optional.

**Cash:** The ticket window (if you're buying at the door) is cash only — no card readers. The official price is 70 MAD per adult. Bring exact change or small bills if you can; staff don't always have change for 200 MAD notes. Even if you've pre-booked online, you'll want cash for the medina shops, street food, and tips.

The nearest ATMs are on or near Rue Riad Zitoun el Kedim. Withdraw before you head into the heart of the medina — chasing an ATM through narrow alleyways while trying to make an opening-time visit adds unnecessary stress.

## 5. Read Up (or Get a Guide) Before You Go

Bahia Palace is stunning to look at. But if you don't know what you're looking at, you'll leave thinking "nice tiles" without understanding the story behind the rooms.

The short version: the palace was built in the late 19th century by Si Moussa, the grand vizier of Sultan Hassan I, and later expanded by his son Ba Ahmed, who became regent after the sultan's death. Ba Ahmed built the palace as a monument to his own power and beauty — the name "Bahia" means "brilliant" or "beautiful" in Arabic. The harem quarters alone housed 24 wives and hundreds of concubines. The scale of ambition is extraordinary for a man who was not even a sultan.

**Options for context:**
- An audio guide is available for hire at the entrance (ask at the ticket area)
- A licensed local guide can be arranged in advance or sometimes at the entrance — prices vary, negotiate beforehand
- Reading a good summary online the night before your visit takes 10 minutes and transforms what you see inside

## 6. Don't Miss the Grand Riad and Harem Courtyard

If you find yourself rushing through the palace, at least slow down in two places:

**The Grand Riad:** The largest open courtyard in the palace, framed by a painted wooden gallery and surrounded by orange trees and flowering plants. It's the spatial heart of the building and one of the most photogenic spots in Marrakech.

**The Harem Courtyard and Chambers:** The private quarters built for Ba Ahmed's wives and concubines. The rooms here are smaller, more intimate, and detailed in ways that the grand reception halls aren't — painted tile dados, carved stucco ceilings at eye level, and a sense of enclosure that makes the scale of the harem suddenly feel very real.

Many visitors move quickly through the outer rooms and miss the depth of what's further inside. Follow the flow of the building through all its sections before turning back.

## 7. Photography Is Allowed — But No Flash

You can photograph freely inside Bahia Palace. There is no photography fee, no restricted area signposting for personal cameras, and staff generally don't intervene with tourists shooting with phones or cameras.

**The one firm rule: no flash.** The painted wooden ceilings, carved stucco, and pigmented tilework are old and light-sensitive. Flash is prohibited to protect them. This is enforced.

**Practical photography tips:**
- The best light for courtyard shots is in the first two hours after opening (9:00–11:00 AM)
- Interior rooms are dimly lit — use your phone's night mode or increase ISO on a camera rather than using flash
- The carved cedar ceiling in the grand reception hall (the Council Room) is best shot directly below — get in position before a tour group arrives
- Reflections in the central fountain of the Grand Riad work well in the early morning before the surface is disturbed by footfall

Tripods may attract attention in crowded areas. If you're a serious photographer, visit early and ask staff if there's any restriction before setting up.

## 8. Watch Out for Tourist Scams Outside

Inside the palace walls, you're safe. Outside is a different story — not because Marrakech is dangerous, but because the area around major tourist sites anywhere in the world attracts people looking to earn money from visitors. Bahia Palace is no exception.

**Common situations to be aware of:**

- **"Unofficial guides"** who offer to show you to the palace entrance (even if you're already standing in front of it) and then expect payment. Politely decline.
- **Men who walk alongside you** offering to "show you the way" through the medina. If you haven't asked for help, this is typically a setup for a tip demand.
- **Henna artists** who offer free application, then insist on an aggressive price once it's on your arm.
- **Shopkeepers** who insist that the site you're looking for has "moved" or is "closed today" and then offer to take you to their shop instead. It hasn't moved. It isn't closed.

None of this is unique to Marrakech. Keep your bearings using Google Maps (download the offline map in advance), walk with purpose, and politely decline unsolicited offers. Most people in the medina are not trying to scam you — but knowing the patterns means you won't be caught off guard.

## 9. Combine Your Visit with Nearby Sites

Bahia Palace sits in the southern medina, and two of Marrakech's other major historical sites are within a short walk:

**Saadian Tombs** (~10 minutes on foot, south of the palace): The royal mausoleum of the Saadian dynasty, featuring intricately decorated chambers around the remains of members of the royal court. Closed under the Alaoui dynasty for centuries, it was only rediscovered in 1917. Fascinating and often included in the same half-day itinerary as Bahia Palace.

**El Badi Palace** (~15 minutes on foot, west of the palace): The ruins of a massive 16th-century palace complex built by Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur. Much of it was stripped of its materials centuries ago, leaving dramatic roofless halls and stork-nested towers. A very different experience from Bahia — more ruinous, more open, worth an hour.

A good half-day itinerary: Start at Bahia Palace at 9:00 AM, spend 1.5 hours inside, then walk to the Saadian Tombs, then end at El Badi Palace before lunch. All three within 4–5 hours total.

---

## 10. Allow 1.5 to 2 Hours — Not More, Not Less

**Don't underestimate it:** Visitors who rush through Bahia Palace in 30–40 minutes typically only see the first two or three courtyards. The further sections — the private apartments, harem quarters, and gardens — reward slower exploration and are where the most detailed work is.

**Don't over-plan it:** Unlike a museum with 50 rooms, Bahia Palace has a natural flow and a clear exit. You won't need 3 hours. If you've already spent 90 minutes and feel like you've seen everything, you probably have.

The sweet spot is **90 minutes**: enough to see every section thoughtfully without rushing, with time to sit in the Grand Riad for a few minutes and actually absorb where you are.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is Bahia Palace worth visiting?**
Yes, without reservation. It's one of the best-preserved examples of Moroccan-Andalusian architecture in the country, with 8,000 square meters of painted ceilings, mosaic courtyards, and carved stucco work. For architecture and history enthusiasts it's a highlight of any Morocco trip. Even for casual visitors, an hour and a half inside is time very well spent.

**Q: Is Bahia Palace suitable for children?**
Yes. There's a lot to look at, space to walk, and the open courtyard gardens are engaging for younger visitors. Children under 7 enter free. The main practical consideration is the walking distance through the medina to reach the palace — bring a stroller if needed, and note that some doorways have raised thresholds that require lifting.

**Q: Can I take photos inside Bahia Palace?**
Yes, personal photography is free and unrestricted throughout the palace. Flash photography is prohibited to protect the painted surfaces and woodwork. Tripods may be questioned by staff in busy areas. Video recording for personal use is generally fine.
